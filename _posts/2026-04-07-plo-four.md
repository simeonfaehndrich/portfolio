## PLO4

> Employ information systems and current technologies to address real-world situations, informed by social and cultural perspectives

For an Information Visualization group project, three other students and I put together a popular science blog communicating the dire destruction of kelp forests, summarizing factors contributing to their decline, why these ecosystems are so valuable, and presenting some of the work being done towards remediation. We told a story interweaving visualizations of key data demonstrating some of the research. We sorted open-source data available from the Canadian government and academic researchers using MS Excel and used QGIS and Tableau to present a cohesive set of graphics (in addition to two infographics).

![Image]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/infovis-deotter.jpg)

My contribution was looking at predation cycles. In short—sea otters prey on urchins, urchins prey on kelp, without sea otters kelp is decimated. Sea otters were extinct on the BC coast due to the fur trade till an Alaska population was reintroduced in the late 1960s. I took sea otter numbers on the BC coast for the last fifty years and graphed the totals I calculated in Tableau, then annotated the graph so it was easily understandable and told a compelling story of revival. But the story wasn’t complete without looking at the historical numbers before Europeans wiped out the rich fur-bearing creatures. For this I looked at more qualitative historical documentation and graphed some of the reference points we have—an interesting exercise in digital history.

![Image]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/infovis-reotter.jpg)

Finally, there weren’t numbers on urchin populations, so instead of a data visualization I created an Infographic with Photoshop that continued the story—visualizing how much sea otters eat, and how kelp forests have been found to be 18.8 times bigger when sea otters were present.

![Image]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/infovis-infographic.jpg)

There were many other aspects of this project I worked on such as calculating polygon areas of historical kelp range using QGIS, and mapping the extirpation of kelp beds due to a heatwave with Tableau. Part of the system and technology we used was embedding interactive Tableau visualizations in our Google Blog.

![Image]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/infovis-barkley.jpg)

Our project as a whole spoke to the climate crisis we are experiencing and how that impacts us locally, as well as the global efforts to use science to ameliorate the emergency. I also touched on Indigenous ecological practices on the coast in writing about the history of sea otters and kelp—First Nations continue to be key to healthier relationships with marine ecosystems.

I took away a sense of how you can use advanced tools to tell fun and compelling stories towards social and environmental justice.
