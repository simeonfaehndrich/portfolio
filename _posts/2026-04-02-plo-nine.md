## PLO9

> Develop respectful, reciprocal relationships with professional and community groups

One of the funnest little developments over my career at the iSchool has been my involvement with a group of volunteer paleographers. The group is called Les Gardenotes, a group of amateur and professional paleographers, genealogists, and historians that volunteer a couple hours each week to transcribe digitized manuscripts and review these as a group. 

The group is based around Quebec, but all the work happens remotely. They are working to create free accessible history which will be launched soon on the platform “La Nouvelle France Numérique” (Digital New France). I first learned about this group through one of the professionals (a professor at UQAR) I had chatted with for the co-op networking assignment. To work on my professional French and develop connections in Quebec (as well as continue to learn about Transkribus HTR), I decided to volunteer last fall.

The group is a mix of experience levels and privileges a mentorship model, thus groups pair those familiar with particular handwriting and the Gardenotes’ standards with new volunteers. The meetings are collegial, where as a rule we tutoye each other (use the informal ‘you’), but rigorous on the high standard of our work, where we question every capital and point of punctuation. We remain respectful by querying commentary in the way we review each others work, and discussing sticky points until everyone feels satisfied. While I don’t have the same background on the historical and regional knowledge, I’ve been able to contribute with my technical skills, awareness of the vagaries and exceptions of French as an outsider (it’s my third language).

This reciprocity in the varied ways we can show up to contribute to a community created source of knowledge has shown me that citizens and volunteer time are a powerful tool in making information free. Some elements that I’m taking away from Les Gardenotes so far is the intentionality in forming small trusting groups, rather than prioritizing anonymous transcription without context such as some other online sites offer (e.g. PapersPast, and FamilySearch). The weekly meetings take a lot of time, but the number of iterations and eyes on the text lead to a better final product that will be useful long into the future.
